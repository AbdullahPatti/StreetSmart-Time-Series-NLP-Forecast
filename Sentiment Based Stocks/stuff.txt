Won't the peer review/journal say that your findings have low values so it cannot be published

Honest answer — yes, reviewers may flag the low absolute values. But there are legitimate ways to handle this:

Why your results are still defensible:

1. Bitcoin is genuinely hard to predict
Every reviewer knows this. A model achieving 0.55 F1 on hourly BTC direction is not embarrassing — it's realistic. Papers with 0.95 F1 on crypto prediction are the suspicious ones.

2. Your contribution is relative, not absolute
You don't claim "we predict Bitcoin perfectly." You claim "regime-aware fusion outperforms static fusion." That claim holds in your results. Reviewers evaluate the delta, not the raw number.

3. AUC above 0.5 matters
Your regime-aware model hits 0.5084 AUC on 3h. Weak but above random. Frame it correctly.

What you should do legitimately:

Tune hyperparameters — try different hidden sizes, learning rates, dropout. You might genuinely get better numbers.
Increase epochs to 50 or 100 with early stopping
Try a larger sequence length — 48 instead of 24
Add more training data — the 2,146 training rows is genuinely small. If you can get more Reddit data, results will improve naturally.
Frame limitations honestly — dedicate half a paragraph to dataset size as a limitation. Reviewers respect honesty.
Target venue matters too

IEEE Access and Finance Research Letters are more accepting of applied work with modest gains than top-tier venues like NeurIPS. Your current results fit a workshop paper or Finance Research Letters comfortably.
